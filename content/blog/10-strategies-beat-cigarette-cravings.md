## Cravings Are Temporary. Every Single One.

Here is a fact that sounds simple but changes everything when you internalize it: **the average cigarette craving lasts 3 to 5 minutes.**

It does not feel that short. In the moment, a craving can feel like an emergency — urgent, overwhelming, impossible to ignore. But the sensation genuinely peaks and subsides within minutes. Every craving you have ever had has eventually passed, whether you smoked or not.

The strategies below work by giving you something to do with those 3-5 minutes. Each one is backed by research. Not all of them will work equally well for you — find your three, and use them.

---

## 1. The 4-7-8 Breathing Technique

Inhale for 4 counts. Hold for 7. Exhale for 8.

This breathing pattern activates your parasympathetic nervous system — the part of your nervous system responsible for rest and calm. A craving is, at its core, a stress response. Controlled breathing short-circuits the physiological alarm.

Do three cycles. By the end of the third, most cravings have significantly weakened.

---

## 2. Delay It by 10 Minutes

Tell yourself you will smoke in 10 minutes. Set a timer.

In most cases, the craving will have passed before the timer goes off. If it has not, delay again. This technique works because it sidesteps the all-or-nothing urgency of a craving. You are not refusing to smoke forever — just for 10 minutes.

---

## 3. Cold Water

Drinking a large glass of cold water slowly, or holding ice in your hands, is surprisingly effective. The physical sensation cuts through the craving's mental noise. It also hydrates you, which reduces headaches and fatigue during withdrawal.

Some people find that sipping water through the moments when they would normally smoke — morning coffee, after meals — replaces the ritual element of smoking without the nicotine.

---

## 4. Physical Movement

A 5-minute walk dramatically reduces craving intensity, according to multiple studies. Exercise releases dopamine naturally — the same neurotransmitter that nicotine artificially stimulates.

You do not need a gym. Stand up. Walk outside. Do ten push-ups. The movement itself is the intervention.

---

## 5. Identify and Name the Trigger

Not all cravings are the same. Some are triggered by stress. Some by boredom. Some by specific places, people, or times of day. Some are purely habitual — your brain expecting a cigarette because you always smoke at this time.

When a craving hits, ask yourself: what triggered this? Naming the trigger does not make the craving disappear, but it shifts you from passive reaction to active observation. That mental distance is valuable.

---

## 6. Nicotine Replacement Therapy

Nicotine patches, gum, lozenges, inhalers — these are not cheating. They are evidence-based medical interventions that double your chances of quitting successfully.

NRT works by providing a slow, steady dose of nicotine that prevents the sharp peaks and crashes associated with smoking. This reduces craving intensity while your brain recalibrates.

If you are not using NRT, consider talking to your doctor or pharmacist about whether it is right for you.

---

## 7. Change Your Environment Immediately

Cravings are often location-specific. If you always smoked on the back porch, the back porch will trigger cravings. The solution is simple: leave.

Walk to a different room. Go outside in a direction you do not associate with smoking. Call someone. Physical environment change interrupts the conditioned response.

---

## 8. Talk to Someone

Calling or texting another person during a craving does two things: it occupies your mind with something other than the craving, and it activates social accountability. Both are powerful.

The research on social support and smoking cessation is consistent: people with strong support networks quit at significantly higher rates than those who try alone.

---

## 9. Remind Yourself Why

Write down your reasons for quitting. Keep them somewhere visible — your phone lock screen, a sticky note on your mirror, the first screen you see in iQuit.

During a craving, your brain narrows focus to the immediate relief smoking would provide. Reading your reasons out loud forces your brain to hold the longer view. Why does staying smoke-free matter to you? Health? Money? Family? Freedom?

The craving is fighting a 3-minute battle. Your reasons represent the rest of your life.

---

## 10. Accept the Discomfort Without Fighting It

This one is counterintuitive but important.

Trying hard to suppress or ignore a craving often amplifies it — a phenomenon known in psychology as ironic process theory (the same principle that makes it impossible to stop thinking about something when you are told not to think about it).

A more effective approach is acceptance: acknowledge the craving without acting on it. "I am having a craving right now. It is uncomfortable. It will pass." This is not passive resignation — it is active, skillful observation.

Mindfulness-based smoking cessation has strong evidence behind it, and it starts with this: let the craving be there, and watch it pass anyway.

---

## Build Your Personal Toolkit

No single strategy works for everyone in every situation. The goal is to build a personal toolkit of 3-4 techniques that work for you, so that when a craving hits, you already know what to do.

Track which techniques help most. Notice your strongest trigger times. Over the first few weeks, you will develop a map of your cravings — where they come from, when they peak, and what reliably gets you through.

Each craving you survive is data. Each one makes the next one easier. Keep going.
